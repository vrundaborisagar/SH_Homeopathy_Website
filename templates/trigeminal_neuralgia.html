Trigeminal Neuralgia (Left & Right)

Trigeminal neuralgia is a chronic pain condition that affects the trigeminal nerve, which carries sensation from your face to your brain. It is characterized by sudden, sharp, electric-shock-like pains on one side of the face — commonly on the left or right — triggered by simple activities like brushing teeth, eating, or even touching the face. The pain can last from a few seconds to a couple of minutes and can come and go in episodes. Over time, episodes may become more frequent and intense, making daily life very difficult for those affected.

Symptoms

  • Severe, sharp, stabbing, or shooting pain on one side of the face
  • Pain triggered by light touch on the face or mouth
  • Pain around the jaw, lips, cheek, or forehead
  • Sudden pain that comes and goes in bursts
  • Pain episodes lasting a few seconds to a couple of minutes
  • Pain worsening while talking, chewing, or brushing teeth
  • In some cases, mild, continuous aching between episodes

What Makes It Worse

  • Sudden facial touch or breeze on the face
  • Chewing or talking for long periods
  • Brushing or shaving the face roughly
  • Cold or very windy weather
  • Stress and mental strain
  • Poor oral hygiene or infections in the mouth or teeth
  • Ignoring early signs and delaying treatment

Precautions

  • Avoid very cold or hot food and drinks that can trigger pain
  • Cover your face properly in cold or windy weather
  • Practice gentle oral hygiene to prevent triggering pain
  • Avoid sudden face movements or extreme facial expressions
  • Keep stress and anxiety under control with relaxation techniques
  • Avoid highly processed or spicy foods that can irritate nerves
  • Get enough rest and follow a healthy lifestyle
