Frozen Shoulder

Frozen Shoulder, also called Adhesive Capsulitis, is a condition where the tissues around the shoulder joint become stiff and tight. This causes pain and a gradual loss of movement, making simple tasks like lifting your arm difficult. It often develops slowly and can last for months or even years if not treated properly.

How to prevent it

  • Staying active and moving the shoulder regularly.
  • Gentle stretching exercises after any shoulder injury or surgery.
  • Avoiding long periods of immobilization or poor posture.
  • Treating shoulder pain promptly so that stiffness doesn’t set in.

Stages of Frozen Shoulder

Stage 1 (Freezing):
The pain in the shoulder gradually worsens over time, especially with movement or at night. The shoulder becomes increasingly stiff and tender, making everyday tasks difficult. This stage can last for several weeks to months as the shoulder slowly “freezes” with reduced range of motion.

Stage 2 (Frozen):
Pain may decrease, but stiffness reaches its peak. The shoulder feels very tight and difficult to move. Activities like reaching up or behind become very hard. This stage can last several months.

Stage 3 (Thawing):
The shoulder gradually regains mobility. Movement improves, pain lessens, and daily activities become easier. This recovery stage may take several months.

Symptoms

  • Gradual onset of shoulder pain and stiffness.
  • Difficulty moving the arm up, to the side, or behind the back.
  • Pain is often worse at night or during rest.
  • Shoulder feels tight with very limited range of motion.

What Makes It Worse

Keeping the shoulder still for too long can allow stiffness to build up. Ignoring early signs of pain and stiffness leads to more restriction. Poor posture adds strain on the joint. Sudden movements or lifting heavy objects may increase inflammation. Stress and lack of gentle exercise can slow down recovery and worsen long-term pain.
